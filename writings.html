General concept and objectives of the project
In 1973, the Canadian composer and theorist R. Murray Schafer stated that the ʻblurring of the edges between music and environmental sounds is the most striking feature of twentieth century musicʼ.
Definition
Certainly the definition of music had been undergoing radical change around that same time. In another contemporaneous definition, John Cage declared: “Music is sounds, sounds heard around us whether weʼre in or out of concert halls”.
Source and ties
From the technological point of view it was sound recording that had altered the very nature of music. With the invention of electroacoustic equipment, music could be detached from both its source and its ties to any particular setting and location.
New sound-shaping and space-making devices
Since the early seventies, were being made available, with which new sonic worlds could be created, working with the plastic, aesthetic and poetic qualities of sounds. In this way the process of making music moved much closer to the process of painting.
Insertions
Since it became possible to insert any sound from the environment into a musical composition, the microphone itself, for many artists, became a musical instrument.
Abstractions and patterns
The practice of audio and sound recording in the outside world (e.g. in a forest, a pedestrian crossing, or an empty space et al.), as opposed to that which is done within the studio environment, is known as ʻfieldrecordingʼ. Field recording has become a medium in a wide range of diverse artistic domains, and is now commonly encountered both as a distinct artistic practice and as a component of music and sound art.
